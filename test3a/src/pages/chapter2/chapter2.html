Figures suggest 92% of people have a firm idea of whom they would like to inherit their estate yet only 48% have made a will, 56% of parents and 70% of cohabiting couples have no will.

2.1 | Why You Should Make a Will

If you die without making a will, or if your will is out of date because of marriage or a change in circumstance, you will die intestate, the law then decides who should deal with the estate, with very specific rules around distribution of assets.

This means people who you wish to inherit your estate may not. This makes everything difficult to sort out, emotions can run high and families sometimes fall out.

If there is no will, then the estate will follow the rules of intestacy. There is little provision in these circumstances for the welfare of children and fairly strict rules over who will get what.

The Rules of Intestacy

The intestacy rules provide that an estate of up to £250k goes to surviving spouse.

If the estate is over £250k, the first £250k plus personal possessions, go to surviving spouse. The rest is divided in two; half goes to children or grandchildren (at 18), the other half is held in trust to pay an income to the surviving spouse.

Upon death of the spouse, the funds go to surviving children or grandchildren. If there are no children or grandchildren and the individual has remarried, then all goes to the surviving spouse.

There is no legal recognition of a common-law spouse, the term does not exist in law, and co-habiting couples can be left without anything. Couples who have lived together for a long time but never married, and never divorced from a previous marriage may find that on the death of a loved one, a former long forgotten spouse inherits everything.

2.2 | Your Details 2.3 | Some of the Facts

Inheritance Tax is 40% on an estate valued over 325K, but if you leave it all to a surviving husband or wife and there is no tax at all. On the second death the exemption rises to 650K. If you do not have a will, the rules state that if there are children, the spouse will not inherit it all, it will be split.

If leaving to a partner he or she may die before you, it is then usual to leave to children, but at what age? If not specified this will be 18, is your 18 year old ready for inheritance? You can choose for them to inherit at any age, perhaps 25 may be better. You can still ensure funds are there for education and other needs before they reach 25.

2.4 | Planning your will

If you do not already have a will, planning will help you think about what is important to you before you meet with your Solicitor. Take time to consider your estate. Your will is a very personal document, bearing in mind other people may see this booklet, we advise that you plan on a separate sheet of paper. Take your plan with you to the Solicitor to make the appointment easier by having clear intentions of what you want.

Draw your family tree, and highlight individuals that you want to have in your will. Think about any brothers or sisters that you might want to provide for, or extended family members that you might want to include. Remember also that step children or step grandchildren are not provided for within the law, if you wish them to inherit you must include them in your will and decide at what age they should receive the benefits.

2.5 | Bequests and legacies

A bequest is a specific gift of an item or property. Think about anything you might like to bequest and to whom. It could be a family heirloom, jewellery or a piece of furniture, in fact anything that you want to give to a specific person.

Legacies or pecuniary bequests are a set amount of money. Think about any individuals or charities to whom you would like to leave a specific sum of money.

Residuary bequests, once your estate has been finalised, and all of your accounts settled, the funds that are left can be distributed as you wish. A residuary bequest is usually a percentage of the estate that you might leave to family members after everything else has been taken into consideration. Make a list of the people that you would like to inherit from you and think about what percentage of your estate you would like them to have.

2.6 | Bequests and legacies

In writing your will you can choose who you would want to raise your children in the event of your death. Whoever you appoint can decline to take it up, if they feel they cannot do it, and a court can override a person’s wishes if it is concerned there is a better alternative.

If you die without a will that names a guardian, anyone wishing to take care of your children can ask a judge for guardianship. The judge will then decide who can raise your children without your input.

2.7 | Executors

An Executor is a personal representative that you name in you will, to pay your taxes and debts, collect and manage your property and distribute your assets in accordance with your will.

2.17 | Making your will:

There are many options for making your will, and a lot of contradictions in advice and guidance, especially on the internet. This is a guide to the options available:

DIY wills

There is a much bigger chance of making a mistake when you draw up your will yourself. If no expert advice is sought then mistakes are easily made, something which may not be discovered until it is too late to do anything about it.

The forms are easily purchased and offer a cheap alternative to using a Solicitor, but only suitable for the simplest of wills.

Online wills

They are convenient and come with limited guidance for completion.

It is still very easy to make mistakes and they are not suitable for complicated arrangements.

There may be an additional fee for an online checking service, but issues could still be missed, as the person checking has limited information about you and your situation and may not pick up any errors.

Will writing services

This is a possible option and may be (but not always) cheaper than going to a Solicitor

You will need to be very clear about what you want, and make sure that they understand your needs. Take a clear written plan with you.

Many will writers are not solicitors, however many do have a professional status and indemnity insurance. It is wise to ask about the level of advice you might be given as they may be less likely to advise than a solicitor.

Banks and building societies

It is not usually a good idea to take up the offer of a free will with your bank or building society. Although they may write your will for free they often push for themselves to be executors, giving them the right to charge large fees.

Sometimes they pass the work out to a solicitor of their choosing, (not yours), taking away your control of the situation. Some banks and building societies use third party End of Life Planners, at which stage there may be costs involved, so it is best to check what exactly they are offering.

Solicitor

This is the best recommendation but shop around as prices vary. A good solicitor can help you think about gifts, legacies, the use of trusts, charitable giving, care home costs and inheritance tax.

Make sure to ask if there are additional costs, for example some companies charge for safe storage of your documents. (If you are leaving your will in there safe keeping take a photocopy to keep with your papers).

It is advisable to check that the Solicitor you choose has the appropriate expertise, don’t assume all Solicitors are experts in everything. Look for Solicitors for the Elderly (SFE), Society of Trust and Estate Practitioners (STEP), Wills and Inheritance Quality Scheme (WIQS), as professionals with membership of these organisations are likely to be more experienced.
