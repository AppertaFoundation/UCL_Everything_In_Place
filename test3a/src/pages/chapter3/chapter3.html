3.1 | Lasting Powers of Attorney

Powers of Attorney refer to the appointment of a person (an ‘attorney’) to make decisions on your behalf. It is well documented that as an aging population that is living longer, there is an increased risk that we will suffer more medical conditions.

In many cases, towards our later years, it is possible there will be more than one condition affecting us, our ability to communicate our wishes and preferred choices may be taken from us, through stroke, dementia or other types of illness.

Powers of Attorney and Advance Care Planning is based around mental capacity.

In the short term it is useful to have a joint bank account with your spouse/partner, at least then cash can be accessed in an emergency. Simple things like making sure both names are on housing documents etc. can save much heartache in the future.


3.2 | Defining mental capacity

The definition of capacity is the ability to make your own decisions. Under UK law capacity is judged according to the specific decision to be made. Someone could have the capacity to make a simple decision, but not more complicated ones.

The Mental Capacity Act (MCA) states that a person lacks capacity to make a decision if they have ‘an impairment or disturbance in the function of their mind or brain’ (either temporary or permanent) and as a result cannot do one or more of the following.


~ Understand the information relating to the decision (benefits and risks)

~ Retain the information long enough to make the decision

~ Weigh up the information involved in the decision making

~ Communicate the decision in any way

Capacity should be checked by whoever wants the particular decision to be made. The principles of Mental Capacity Law state:


- You must assume that the person does have capacity to make their own decision unless there is clear evidence that they do not.

- You must take all reasonable steps to help the person make their own decision before you conclude they lack capacity to do so.

- The fact that someone has made an unwise decision does not, in itself mean that they lack capacity. (We all make mistakes).

- Anything you do on behalf of someone who lacks capacity must be in their best interest.

- Consider whether you can achieve the same result in a less restrictive way.


3.3 | Best interest decisions

A ‘best interest’ decision is not defined in law, but the MCA lists some factors you must take into account when deciding what is in someone’s best interest. These include:


Whether the decision can wait until the person regains capacity.

Involving the incapacitated person in the decision making if possible.

Consider their beliefs and values, what would they have wanted in the past.

Find out opinions of family and close friends.


3.4 | Enduring Powers of Attorney

In the past, an Enduring Power of Attorney (EPA) was advised, allowing someone to make decisions on your behalf and take over the responsibility of your financial affairs. However, on 1st October 2007 the EPA was replaced with the Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA).

EPAs signed prior to that date are still valid and can be registered but the LPA is far more flexible and you have the option of taking out either a Property and Financial Affairs LPA or a Health and Welfare LPA, or both. EPA’s must be registered before they can be used and apply to property and finance only.


3.5 | LPA for financial decisions

The LPA for financial decisions allows you (the donor) to appoint an attorney, (or more than one), to manage your property, finances and affairs when today you have the capacity to make your own decisions but remains valid if you lose that capacity. You can limit it to only be used when you lack capacity, i.e. if you can manage things yourself then you continue do so. It also offers the option of giving your attorney the power to make decisions about part, or all, of your property and financial affairs.

This includes everything from paying bills, managing bank accounts etc., to overseeing the sale of your home, for example some of the decisions that can be made:


- Buying or selling property

- Operating bank accounts and investing decisions

- Giving access to the donor’s financial information

- Receiving any income inheritance or claiming benefits and pensions

- Dealing with the donor’s tax affairs

- Paying the donor’s mortgage, rent and household expenses

- Insuring, maintaining and repairing the donor’s property

- Making limited gifts on the donor’s behalf

- Paying for private medical care and residential or nursing home fees


3.6 | LPA for health and care decisions

A health and care LPA allows you to appoint an attorney to make decisions on your be- half in respect of health care and welfare. Making decisions about your day to day health care needs from what you wear to how your medical treatment is delivered.

If there are any circumstances where you would want your attorney to refuse life sustaining treatment, this needs to be clearly identified and included in the documents.

The LPA for health and care can cover:


~ Where the donor should live

~ How the donor’s day to day care is managed

~ Who the donor may have contact with

~ Consenting or refusing medical treatments

~ Assessments and provision of community care services

~ Donor taking part in social activities, leisure or education

~ Manage the donor’s personal correspondence and papers

~ Access to personal information about donor

~ Complaints about the donor’s care or treatment


You can have the same attorney or attorneys appointed for each LPA, but if you appoint different ones in each, then only health and care attorneys can make health care decisions (and vice versa), both sets of forms need to be registered.

Unlike the EPA, the new LPA requires that the person making the LPA is certified to have the mental capacity to do so, confirming they are preparing and signing the document without being subjected to any pressure or fraud. The certificate provider or witness of the new LPA must complete a statement to that effect, stating that he or she has discussed the LPA with the person making the LPA and is satisfied the person understands the scope and purpose of the LPA, and is under no undue pressure to make it.

3.8 | Who can be an attorney

The role of attorney involves a great deal of power and responsibility, it is very important that you choose the right person. Your attorney could be a family member, friend, spouse, partner or civil partner or someone you know well.

However if you are using a professional, it is really important to check with them whether they would charge a fee to address any issues that might arise in the future.

For example if a decision is needed regarding choosing a care home, a Solicitor can charge a normal hourly rate whilst carrying out this function, realistically this could be many hours. Your attorney can claim back any expenses they incur as a result of their role as your attorney, as long as they keep accounts and receipts so it is important to consider whether appointing a professional may incur large costs and eventually devalue your estate.

~ It is usual to appoint two (or more) attorneys – in case one is unable to carry out the role. Discuss your request with the individuals that you think might be suitable to act on your behalf and make sure they understand the responsibility they are taking on.

~ Your attorney can be anyone aged 18 years or over, family, friend, professional, spouse or partner.

~ If you choose your spouse and the relationship breaks down and you are then divorced the LPA will not be valid, unless you have included a clause that says in such an event that person can continue to act on your behalf or you appoint a replacement attorney.

~ You cannot appoint anyone who lacks mental capacity.

~ You cannot appoint anyone who is an undischarged bankrupt.

~ If you have more than one child it may be worth appointing them jointly for all decisions, then one cannot do anything without the others consent – you know your family best. A common approach would be spouse first then children.

~ Your attorneys can work jointly or severally, or jointly and severally, this is the term used to describe whether they work separately or together, or only jointly for some decisions, it is up to you to decide what would be best for you.


You should choose someone you trust, someone that knows your beliefs and who would make sure that you and your finances were well cared for. It might be a good idea to appoint the same person to be the Executor of your will, as the LPA ceases to exist on death and the will takes over.

3.10 | Planning for your LPA

An LPA is by the far the easiest (legal way) of giving someone else responsibility for your decisions. Without appointing an LPA you could be left in a position where your accounts will be frozen and nothing released until the Court of Protection can appoint someone, this is a costly and time consuming process. (So please think carefully especially if you own property.)

If you have not yet prepared your LPA, it is wise to have conversations with the people that you would like to act on your behalf? You should ask them if they are willing to take on this role, talk things through with them about your expectations and what they would need to do. You need to be sure they understand your wishes. Think about the following whilst planning your LPA.

Things to Consider

Consider whether you may need to appoint replacement attorneys in case anything should happen to your original choices. If your appointed attorneys become unable to act, or if they are only allowed to make decisions jointly and one becomes unable to act, the other cannot act alone. If you do not have any replacements, then you would have to go through the whole process of appointing new ones. This would incur additional costs, but would not be possible if you had already lost capacity.

Consider whether you would like to impose a condition where the LPA is not valid unless mental capacity is lost. The health LPA is only valid if and when capacity is lost. However with the Financial LPA, you will need to decide when the LPA should become effective. If you decide it should be when capacity is lost, it is important to understand that defining loss of capacity can be difficult to prove, it may mean that your attorneys cannot act immediately as you would wish. If you trust your attorneys, (as you should) it may be better to register the document to become valid immediately, but in reality for you to continue making your own decisions, then if and when capacity is lost the attorneys can step in immediately.

Consider whether you might want your health LPA to refuse life sustaining treatment on your behalf. It would never be an easy decision for anyone to make, but giving someone permission now to switch of a machine or refuse an organ transplant, may help relieve the guilt they could feel later; by knowing it was what you would have wanted.

You also need to decide whether attorneys should act:


~ Jointly – meaning they work together on all matters

~ Jointly and severally – where they may act together or separately

~ A mixture of both depending on the type of decision to be made

You must specify in the LPA documents how you want the attorneys to act; maybe jointly for specific decisions such as selling a house and jointly and severally for smaller day to day decisions regarding your care. If you have more than one child it maybe worth appointing them jointly for all decisions, then one cannot act without the other’s consent, you know your family best.


3.11 | Office of the Public Guardian

Once you have prepared your forms, you have a couple of options:

You can put the forms to one side but it is not valid until it is registered, the process of registration can take up to 10 weeks.

If you do not register an LPA of either type and lose mental capacity then the position can become very complicated. Nobody can act for you and generally everything is frozen until a Court of Protection Order has been obtained. This is an expensive process and the Court controls decisions on the release of capital, which can be very stressful and time consuming for the family.

The recommended option is to register the document immediately after it is signed, as everything is then in place. The fee is currently £110 for each Lasting Power of Attorney (with a reduction if in receipt of certain benefits). You can decide whether the financial LPA comes into force straight away or whether you put a clause in that you only want it to become active if and when you lose mental capacity. The Health LPA only becomes active if and when capacity is lost.

You do not need a lawyer for this, both types of forms are on the Office of the Public Guardian website, they are simple to complete and there is excellent guidance. Further information can be sought from the Office of the Public Guardian (OPG).
